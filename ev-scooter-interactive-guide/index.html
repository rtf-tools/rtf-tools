Core Buying Questions: Answered Honestly

The questions every buyer actually has, with answers from owner data — not manufacturer brochures.

Two chassis, four trim combinations. The naming encodes specs: first digits = battery in 0.1 kWh, last two = trim level.

Chetak C2501 — genuinely different. Compact chassis (1,225 mm wheelbase vs 1,355 mm), telescopic front fork, 25L boot, 107 kg, front disc brake standard. City-sized platform. ₹84,500 post-subsidy.

Chetak 3001 — replaces the discontinued 2903. Same 35-series body, 3.1 kW hub motor, 127 km IDC, drum brakes, no fast charging. The new volume seller at ₹94,500.

Chetak 3501 — top trim. 4.2 kW motor, 73 km/h, 153 km IDC, TFT screen, onboard fast charger, keyless FOB. But: Sport mode, Hill Hold, navigation are TecPac-locked (₹4,000–10,000 extra). ₹1,17,500 post-subsidy before TecPac.

Verdict: The 3001 is the sweet spot — 35-series build, no TecPac paywall confusion, 95 km real range. The C2501 is for tight parking urban use. The 3501 buyer should budget ₹5,000–12,000 extra for TecPac to unlock what they paid for.

Service reach decides this, not specs. For buyers in Nagpur, Indore, Coimbatore, Jaipur, Visakhapatnam: Chetak wins because Bajaj has 2,700+ touchpoints vs Ather's ~500 (concentrated in metros).

A software glitch on an Ather in a city without a service centre means days of downtime waiting for transport to the nearest hub. A Chetak with a mechanical issue can at minimum have its metal body repaired at any local workshop.

Where Ather wins: Real-world range (10–20 km more), Ather Grid fast-charging network (4,357 points in 370+ cities), better battery warranty (8 yr/80k km with Pro Pack), superior software features when they work.

Where Chetak wins: Service ubiquity, metal build quality, simpler software with fewer bugs, lower non-warranty repair costs, better resale in smaller cities.

Verdict: Chetak for anyone outside the top 10 metros. Ather for buyers in Bengaluru, Mumbai, Delhi, Hyderabad, Chennai, Pune who can access Ather Space service and the Grid network routinely.

Magnus Neo and Magnus Grand are mechanically near-identical. Same 2.3 kWh LFP pack, same hub motor, same 65 km/h top speed, same 22L boot. The Grand adds marginally better LCD cluster, premium seat, and new colours — worth ₹3,000–5,000 extra maximum.

Both struggle with a pillion on flyovers. Hub motor overheats at sustained full throttle for 20+ minutes. Real-world range 50–75 km — honest commuter range for sub-30 km daily rides. Not the 80–90 km Ampere markets.

Nexus ST is a genuinely different product — 3.0 kWh LFP, mid-mounted PMSM, 93 km/h, disc front brake, 7-inch TFT, 3.3 hr fast charging, 24L boot. Real-world range 80–105 km. Competes directly with Chetak 3501 and Rizta S, not with the Magnus series.

Verdict: Skip Magnus Neo unless budget is the absolute constraint (sub-₹85k). Grand over Neo only if you care about aesthetics. Nexus ST at ₹1,24,900 is the real buy in the Ampere lineup — LFP battery, highway-capable speed, 5-year warranty.

For Indian conditions, yes — materially.

LFP (Ampere models): Significantly harder to cause thermal runaway. 2,000+ charge cycles vs 800–1,000 for NMC. More tolerant of 45°C Indian summers. Heavier battery for same kWh. Ampere's 5-year/75,000 km warranty backs this with genuine chemistry confidence.

NMC (Bajaj, Ather 3.7): Higher energy density = lighter battery for same range. More heat-sensitive — requires better thermal management. Most documented 2022 Indian EV fire incidents involved NMC chemistry under thermal stress. With good BMS (Ather) the risk is managed; with poor BMS, it's a real concern.

The practical implication: If you fast-charge frequently, live in a hot climate, or park in direct sun — LFP is meaningfully safer and more durable. If you primarily home-charge overnight and your route is under 60 km, NMC in a well-managed system (Ather) is also fine.

Verdict: For Rajasthan, Gujarat, Central India summers — LFP is the right call. For temperate climates with disciplined overnight charging, NMC in a quality system is acceptable.

PM e-Drive provides ₹5,000 per vehicle for electric two-wheelers priced under ₹1.5 lakh ex-factory. It was reduced from ₹10,000 on 1 April 2025 and extended to 31 July 2026 (from March 2026 original end date).

The ₹5,000 is already baked into every Amazon.in listed price for eligible models — you do not need to claim it separately. The listing price is the post-subsidy price.

22.12 lakh vehicles had been sold under the scheme against a 24.79 lakh target as of early 2026 — meaning the allocation could exhaust before 31 July if sales accelerate.

EOX E2 is ineligible — no RTO registration = no VAHAN subsidy generation.

Verdict: Don't treat ₹5,000 as a meaningful financial reason to buy now vs later. It's a minor incentive. The real financial case for an EV is the ₹1.3–1.5 lakh in fuel savings over 5 years vs a petrol scooter.
